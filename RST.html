Role + Team

BJ Fogg is the self-proclaimed expert in Behavior Design and leads the Behavior Design Lab at Stanford. He accepted 16 students to help him tackle his next project by teaching us his methods. I acted as the user experience designer which primarily included prototyping and testing the product.

Challenge

People are increasingly spending hours upon hours looking at screens. Research has shown numerous negative effects on human health. BJ decided he wanted to utilize behavior design to match individuals with behaviors that would best help them decrease their screentime.

Outcome

We created a website filled with hundreds of potential behaviors that fit individual's specific needs. It reached thousands of people thanks to BJ's twitter following. The Behavior Design Lab decided to continue iterating on this project. See the website here.

Research and Pretotyping

I completed background research to determine what solutions already exist and helped the team to create a database with categorization for matching guidelines.


I also ideated on form factor and onboarding, and pretotyped to get user data. We used two different forms to gather data from users, one long and one short.

Empathize

Choosing User Group, Following Constraints

This survey data allowed us to consider more about who our target user group would be: mothers and working millenials seemed most in need. Behavior design principles guided us to solve for those who were most motivated - if we can’t find a solution that works in changing their behavior, it’s unlikely we can for anyone else.

We were racing against time to get the product out, but we also wanted highly motivated users. These two things came to a head as the highly motivated users would put more time and effort into getting the right solution whereas those less engaged would prefer quicker and more easily engineered methods of reaching their solutions

Prototype

Wireframing

I created the following sketch, which the design team decided to move forward with. We made some changes, created it in Google Sheets, and tested with users.

This approach set out to make users with behavior solutions in two ways: a survey and focus mapping (a behavior design method). The survey answers would allow us to narrow down our solutions to only relevant ones and the focus mapping would give users a chance to involve themselves in the process and pick something they feel is effective and that they can get themselves to do it. This would increase the feeling of investment in the solution and would hopefully increase the chances they try the solution. A mix of both these methods would be offered and a user could be on either extreme, only doing one or the other. This allows us to help users with varying levels of motivation.

Test

Snaptesting

We then pushed ourselves to see how fast we could make the process since this was a big thing stopping people from wanting to participate. To do this we did quite a few less than 15 minute tests with lots of people and varied our methods of reaching and prescribing solutions.

We varied number of solutions, whether they were given or user-brainstormed, focus mapped or binary decision-making to find what was more or ess useful and successful.

Iteration
