    Stanford Neurodiversity Project



    Spring 2019 - 10 weeks

Island in the Sky, Canyonlands National Park, UT

October 20, 2018

Role + Team

The Stanford Neurodiversity Project (SNP), a new program at the time, engaged myself and 3 others. I led the team through the d.school design process, and together we learned about and implemented Universal Design principles.

Challenge

SNP works to help students with cognitive and neurological disabilities thrive at Stanford, and increase awareness. My interest in mental health led our team to dig into mental illness issues related to the community at Stanford.

Outcome

The result of this project was a high fidelity prototype of training materials for student staffers (e.g. resident assistants) to inform them how to make spaces more inclusive. Later, our work was actually used in university student staff training.

Interview Insights

What they felt: Difficulty in making friends

  • I had “one half-friend” in college.
  • “Friends happen to me.”

What they did: Structured social groups

  • Sociology alliance: “I considered those people my friends.”
  • Thursday afternoon story-reading: “with rules”
  • “Conversation happens naturally with somebody when I’m grounded in a visceral experience.”
  • People are afraid their emotions (or symptoms of their mental illness) will be judged or misunderstood as controllable.

What they wanted: To be understood by themselves and by others

  • I am on a “journey of increasing self-awareness.”
  • “I get the feeling that this person wants to find out about my talents.”

Needs

Problems from the literature:

  • Suicide rates: 9x higher than general population’s
  • Access: Being perceived as “too complicated” to be helped, professional misperceptions, support is geared toward children
  • Assessments: Those for depression have not been thoroughly evaluated for efficacy in adults with autism
  • Psychotherapy: Requires specific adaptation for individuals with autism

Brainstormed needs after interviewing people across the neurodiversity spectrum:

  • Accessible mental health activities
     - e.g. nonverbal, low-sensory
  • Small close friend groups
      - Small close friend groups
  • A way to get in touch with their emotions
  • Therapists or resources to have knowledge about neurodiversity
  • Support from a non-professional / people who care about them
  • Strength-based therapies
      - CBT is training neurodiverse individuals to be more neurotypical?
  • Structure that by nature results in friendships
  • Neurotypicals to understand how to interact with them
  • To interact nonverbally

Define

Converged on a level of mental illness prevention


We chose a primary prevention project due to group interest and experience, and realized most of our brainstormed needs were related to this as well. Ultimately we determined a point of view to move forward with:

{need} Stanford neurodiverse students need wellness and social programming that leverage their strengths and interests to prevent social isolation and protect against emergent mental illness and suicidal ideation.
{insight} Unexpectedly, in their world, nonverbal interaction in social settings, such as meditation and focused interest groups, can both bring comfort and create opportunities for self-awareness, while providing structure to instances of shared physical presence.

Ideate

How Might We's

With user needs and a point of view clearly defined, we moved to ideation. We initially landed on three How Might We (HMW) statements we determined were most interesting. However, after looking further into solutions spaces we landed on something more tractable. See all four below, with the rejected statements accompanied by our reasoning against them:

  • HMW #1: Increase self-awareness for individual wellness
     Requires training/resources to create curriculum and structure
  • HMW #2: Form new student groups to prevent social isolation
     Concerns about scalability and sustainability
  • HMW #3: Make existing student organizations more accessible
     Concerns about flexibility (students organizations are diverse in programming)
  • New HMW: Build inclusive, accessible residential communities in college
